QUICK CONTEXT (2-minute read)

Ironclad Distribution is a mid-market third-party logistics company headquartered in Memphis, approximately 600 employees. Natalie Reeves is their VP of Supply Chain and engaged with your content roughly three weeks ago by downloading the warehouse automation ROI whitepaper -- this means she is actively researching solutions, not passively browsing. Your BDR Marcus had a live conversation with her last week where she disclosed two critical pieces of intelligence: (1) they are tracking exception rates manually in spreadsheets, which becomes unsustainable during peak season, and (2) they are running an aging Oracle WMS that she described as "good enough for the basics" -- a phrase that signals she knows it is not good enough for the complex work. The most significant data point is that Ironclad lost a retail client, Redline Sports, last quarter due to fulfillment accuracy issues. That client loss transforms this from an efficiency conversation into a revenue-at-risk conversation. Natalie is not exploring out of curiosity; she is under pressure.

YOUR GOAL FOR THIS CALL

Confirm the operational pain (manual exception tracking, fulfillment accuracy) is tied to measurable business impact (client loss, revenue churn, peak-season capacity), identify who else is involved in evaluating a solution (IT, CFO, COO), and determine whether there is a timeline or triggering event driving urgency. A successful call ends with a second meeting booked that includes a technical stakeholder or economic buyer.

DISCOVERY QUESTIONS -- USE IN THIS ORDER

Opening (earn the right to ask questions):

"Natalie, thanks for making the time. Marcus mentioned you're dealing with exception tracking in spreadsheets that gets particularly painful during peak season -- before I say anything about ShipSmart, I'd love to hear you describe what that actually looks like day-to-day for your team."

Depth questions (once top pain is identified):

1. "When you say it's killing you during peak season -- what does that look like in practice? How many people are touching those spreadsheets, and what happens when something falls through the cracks?"

2. "The Redline Sports situation -- without getting into anything sensitive, was that a one-time issue or is fulfillment accuracy a systemic concern you're seeing across other accounts too?"

3. "You mentioned Oracle WMS is 'good enough for the basics' -- what's the gap between what Oracle handles and what your team is stitching together manually? Where does the system stop and the spreadsheets start?"

4. "Have you evaluated other solutions to close that gap, or is this the first time you're looking outside your current stack? What made now the right time?"

5. "If you could get exception visibility and fulfillment accuracy solved in the next two quarters, what does that change for Ironclad -- is this about keeping existing clients, winning new ones, or both?"

Qualification questions (must know before close):

- "Who else at Ironclad would need to be part of evaluating something like this -- does IT own the WMS relationship, and who makes the final call on spend?"
- "Is there a budget cycle or planning window I should be aware of, or could this move on its own timeline if the case is strong enough?"
- "What's your realistic timeline for addressing this -- are you looking to have something in place before next peak season?"

ANTICIPATED OBJECTIONS AND RESPONSES

Objection 1: "Our Oracle WMS works fine for the basics -- we just need to fix the exception tracking piece, not rip and replace."
-> Response: "That makes total sense, and honestly most of our customers aren't looking to rip and replace either. ShipSmart sits on top of your existing WMS -- it doesn't replace Oracle, it fills the execution gaps Oracle was never designed to cover. The question is really whether the gap between what Oracle does and what your team needs can be closed with spreadsheets, or whether it needs a purpose-built layer. What's your sense of that?"

Objection 2: "We're early in this process -- not ready to commit to anything or bring other people in yet."
-> Response: "Completely fair. At this stage I'm not asking for commitment -- I'm trying to understand whether what you're dealing with is something ShipSmart is genuinely built to solve, or whether your time is better spent elsewhere. If it does look like a fit after today, the next step would just be a working session where we map ShipSmart's capabilities to your specific Oracle environment -- no pressure beyond that. Does that feel reasonable?"

Objection 3: "We've been burned by implementations before -- our team doesn't have bandwidth for a big rollout."
-> Response: "That's a real concern, especially in a 3PL where you can't slow down operations to deploy new tech. It would actually be useful to understand what 'burned' looked like in the past -- was it timeline, integration complexity, or change management? That way if we do move forward, we can design around whatever went wrong before."

CALL SUCCESS CRITERIA

Primary: Natalie confirms the pain is tied to measurable business impact (client churn, revenue, or SLA penalties), identifies other stakeholders involved in evaluation (IT, economic buyer), shares a timeline or triggering event, and agrees to a second meeting with a technical or budget stakeholder present.

Secondary: Pain is confirmed with enough specificity to build a business case, and Natalie agrees to a follow-up call (even if she is the only attendee) with a defined next step.

Minimum: You understand the full scope of the problem (not just exception tracking but the downstream business consequences), and Natalie does not go dark -- she leaves with a clear reason to continue the conversation.

COMPETITORS TO LISTEN FOR

- **Manhattan Associates**: Enterprise-grade WMS/WES. If mentioned, note that Manhattan is powerful but typically targets larger operations and requires significant implementation investment. Position ShipSmart as purpose-built for the mid-market execution layer without the enterprise overhead.
- **Blue Yonder (JDA)**: Similar to Manhattan -- heavy, enterprise-focused. If mentioned, ask what stage they are in with Blue Yonder and whether implementation complexity is a concern.
- **Körber (HighJump)**: Common in 3PL space. If mentioned, ask what they liked and didn't like -- Körber is often strong on WMS but weaker on real-time execution visibility.
- **Homegrown/spreadsheet expansion**: The most likely "competitor" here. If Natalie suggests they could just build better spreadsheets or hire more people, redirect to the Redline Sports loss -- manual processes failed under pressure and the cost was a client, not just inefficiency.
- **Oracle WMS upgrades**: If Natalie mentions upgrading Oracle, ask whether Oracle's roadmap addresses the execution-layer gaps or just the basics they already have covered. The question is whether Oracle is investing in the exception-management and real-time accuracy layer Ironclad needs.

THINGS NOT TO DO

1. **Do NOT lead with a product demo or feature walkthrough.** This is a 30-minute discovery call. If you spend time showing ShipSmart, you will not learn what you need to qualify this deal. Discovery first; demo later.

2. **Do NOT dwell on the Redline Sports loss.** Reference it once to establish stakes, then let Natalie control how much she shares. Pressing on a lost client can feel like you're poking a wound to create urgency -- she already feels the urgency.

3. **Do NOT badmouth Oracle.** Natalie called it "good enough for the basics" -- that's her assessment. If you attack Oracle, you risk insulting a decision she (or her team) made. Position ShipSmart as complementary, not a replacement.

4. **Do NOT ask more than 2-3 questions in a row without offering something back.** Discovery calls that feel like interrogations kill momentum. After every 2-3 questions, share a brief relevant insight or validate what you've heard before asking the next question.

5. **Do NOT end the call without proposing a specific next step with a date.** "Let's stay in touch" is not an outcome. Propose a specific follow-up: "Would it make sense to do a 45-minute working session next week where we map this to your Oracle environment? Who should be in that room?"
