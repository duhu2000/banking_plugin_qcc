# IDEAL CUSTOMER PROFILE -- AI Contract Review Tool

## FIRMOGRAPHIC CRITERIA

**Company size:** 300 -- 1,500 employees
**Revenue range:** $50M -- $500M
**Stage:** Growth-stage / Pre-IPO (Series B or C funded, or recently acquired/merged)
**Geography:** Primary: United States, United Kingdom, Canada. Secondary: EU markets with English-language legal operations, Australia
**Industry (ranked by expected conversion rate):**

1. Fintech -- regulated environment creates high contract volume and urgency around compliance
2. Healthtech -- vendor agreements, BAAs, and data processing agreements multiply contract load
3. B2B SaaS -- rapid growth creates procurement sprawl; vendor management becomes a bottleneck
4. Insurtech -- adjacent to fintech with similar regulatory contract density
5. Regulated marketplaces -- two-sided platforms with supplier agreements at scale

**Technology maturity:** Mid-tech -- the company has adopted cloud tools and modern SaaS, but legal operations still rely on manual review, shared drives, or basic document management. They may have experimented with a CLM but have not built a dedicated legal ops function.

**Legal team profile:** 3 -- 8 lawyers or legal professionals. Large enough to have process pain, small enough that they cannot build internal tooling or hire dedicated legal ops staff.

**Contract volume:** 200+ contracts processed per month (the pain threshold where manual review becomes unsustainable).

## NEGATIVE FIT SIGNALS -- THE ANTI-ICP

### Anti-ICP Profile 1: The Enterprise Evaluator (Engages but never buys)

**Company type:** Fortune 500 or large enterprise with 5,000+ employees and $1B+ revenue
**Why they engage:** Genuinely interested in AI for legal; will attend demos, request detailed security reviews, run pilots
**Why they never close:** They already have a dedicated Legal Ops team (5+ people), an incumbent CLM platform (Ironclad, Icertis, Agiloft), and established processes. Your tool becomes a "nice to have" layered on top of existing workflows. Procurement cycles exceed 6-12 months. Multiple approval layers kill momentum.

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Legal Ops team of 5+ with a dedicated Director/VP of Legal Ops
- Existing CLM platform in their tech stack
- RFP process with 20+ evaluation criteria
- Request for SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, and custom security questionnaire before first demo
- Evaluation committee with 6+ stakeholders
- "We want to run a 90-day pilot with a subset of contracts"

**BDR action:** Disqualify after discovery call if 3+ red flags are present. Do not invest more than one demo and one follow-up. If they insist on a long evaluation, move to nurture-only cadence -- do not assign AE time.

### Anti-ICP Profile 2: The Pre-Seed Solo Lawyer (Wrong stage entirely)

**Company type:** Pre-seed to Seed stage startup with fewer than 50 employees, sub-$5M revenue, 1 lawyer (often fractional or outsourced)
**Why they engage:** The solo lawyer or founder is curious about AI tools. May sign up for a free trial.
**Why they never close:** Contract volume is too low to justify the cost (under 20 contracts/month). The solo lawyer can handle the load manually. No budget allocated for legal tooling -- every dollar goes to product and GTM. Decision-maker is the CEO, and legal tooling is never a priority at this stage.

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Company has fewer than 50 employees
- Legal function is outsourced to a law firm or handled by a single part-time counsel
- No dedicated legal line item in the budget
- Crunchbase shows Pre-Seed or Seed with sub-$5M raised
- Founder is the one requesting the demo (not a legal professional)

**BDR action:** Disqualify immediately. Add to marketing nurture list for when they reach Series A/B. Do not assign AE time or demo slots.

---

### Anti-ICP Profile 3: The Churn Risk (Buys but does not retain)

**Company type:** Mid-market company that matches firmographics but has low contract volume (under 100/month) or is buying for a one-time event (single M&A integration, one-time regulatory audit)
**Why they buy:** Acute short-term pain drives a fast purchase decision.
**Why they churn:** Once the event is resolved, ongoing contract volume does not justify continued spend. ROI story collapses at renewal.

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Buyer frames the need as a "project" rather than an ongoing operational requirement
- Contract volume is seasonal or event-driven, not steady-state
- Champion leaves the company within 6 months of purchase (common in M&A scenarios)
- No plan to expand usage beyond the initial use case

**BDR action:** Qualify carefully on steady-state volume during discovery. If monthly contract volume is under 100 outside of the trigger event, consider offering a short-term engagement or de-prioritise for AE assignment.

# BUYER PERSONAS

## BUYER PERSONA: The Drowning Senior Counsel (Champion)

**Role:** Senior Legal Counsel / Senior Contracts Attorney / Legal Operations Manager
================================================================

### WHO THEY ARE

A mid-career legal professional (7-15 years of experience) who has moved from a law firm to an in-house role for better work-life balance -- only to find themselves buried in contract review. They manage the day-to-day contracting process: reviewing NDAs, vendor agreements, customer contracts, and amendments. They report to the General Counsel or VP Legal. They are technically competent and open to technology, but they are not a technologist -- they need tools that work out of the box. They are the person who feels the pain most acutely because they are doing the actual review work.

### WHAT THEY WANT

**Primary goal:** Reduce the time from contract submission to execution. They are measured on turnaround time and the number of contracts processed without legal risk.
**Secondary goal:** Demonstrate to leadership that they can scale the legal function without proportional headcount growth. Being the person who "modernised legal ops" is a career accelerator.

### WHAT THEY FEAR

**Primary fear:** A missed clause or non-standard term slipping through review that creates liability for the company. Being the person who "let that contract through" is career-damaging.
**Risk aversion:** Concerned that AI will make errors they are professionally responsible for. Needs to trust the tool's accuracy before advocating internally. Worried about being seen as trying to "replace lawyers with robots" by peers.

### HOW THEY BUY

**Discovery:** Peer recommendations in legal ops communities (CLOC, ACC, in-house counsel Slack groups). LinkedIn content from legal tech thought leaders. Legal tech conference demos. They do NOT respond well to cold email that is generic.
**Evaluation:** Hands-on trial with their own contracts. They want to see the tool handle their specific contract types (not a generic demo). They trust G2 reviews from peers in similar-sized legal teams.
**Decision:** They are the champion, not the final decision-maker. They build the internal business case and present to the GC or CFO. They need ROI data (hours saved, risk reduction) to make their case.
**Blocker:** "What if it misses something critical?" -- accuracy concerns are the number one blocker. Secondary: integration with existing tools (DocuSign, Google Drive, SharePoint).

### HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH THEM

**Tone:** Peer-level, empathetic, practical. They are a professional who is overwhelmed -- acknowledge that without being patronising.
**Language:** "Contract turnaround time," "review bottleneck," "risk flagging," "redline automation," "clause library." They speak legal-operations language, not sales language.
**Avoid:** "AI will replace your legal team" (existential threat). "Just upload your contracts" (oversimplifies their workflow). "Our AI is 99% accurate" (unsubstantiated claims trigger legal-trained scepticism). Generic legaltech buzzwords without substance.
**Lead with:** Peer reference from a similar company ("Here's how [Similar Company]'s legal team cut review time by 60%") or a specific pain point they have publicly expressed.

### WHERE TO REACH THEM

**Primary:** LinkedIn (they are active in legal ops groups). Email (if personalised to a specific trigger). Legal tech conferences (CLOC Institute, ACC Annual Meeting, Legalweek).
**Content:** They read Artificial Lawyer, Legaltech News, CLOC community posts, Above the Law (in-house section), and follow Richard Susskind, Mark Cohen, and legal ops practitioners on LinkedIn.
**Timing:** Most receptive mid-week (Tuesday-Thursday). Avoid month-end and quarter-end when contract volume peaks. Best timing is immediately after a trigger event (funding, M&A, new hire).

## BUYER PERSONA: The Strategic General Counsel (Economic Buyer)

**Role:** General Counsel / Chief Legal Officer
================================================================

### WHO THEY ARE

A senior legal executive (15-25+ years of experience) who oversees the entire legal function. They have a small team (3-8 people) and are responsible for legal risk, compliance, and operational efficiency. They report to the CEO and sit on or near the executive team. They are strategic thinkers who are less concerned with individual contract reviews and more focused on scaling the function, managing outside counsel spend, and protecting the company from legal exposure. They are under pressure from the CEO and board to "do more with less."

### WHAT THEY WANT

**Primary goal:** Scale the legal function to support company growth without proportional headcount increases. They are measured on legal department cost as a percentage of revenue, contract cycle time, and absence of material legal incidents.
**Secondary goal:** Position the legal team as a business enabler rather than a bottleneck. Demonstrate to the CEO and board that legal operations are modern and efficient.

### WHAT THEY FEAR

**Primary fear:** A material legal risk that was preventable -- a missed liability clause, a compliance gap, a data breach caused by a poorly reviewed vendor agreement. This is career-ending at the GC level.
**Risk aversion:** Adopting a tool that creates more problems than it solves. Concerned about: data security (contracts contain the company's most sensitive information), accuracy (professional liability), and vendor lock-in. They have been burned by legal tech vendors who over-promised.

### HOW THEY BUY

**Discovery:** Board-level peer networks (other GCs), executive roundtables, recommendations from outside counsel. They do NOT browse G2 or respond to BDR emails. They rely on their team (the Senior Counsel champion) to surface and vet tools.
**Evaluation:** They review the business case presented by their champion. They want: ROI projections, security posture (SOC 2, data handling), reference calls with GCs at peer companies, and a clear implementation timeline.
**Decision:** They are the economic buyer. They approve the budget. For purchases under $50K/year, they may decide unilaterally. Above that, they may need CFO co-sign. The champion's enthusiasm and a strong peer reference are the two strongest closing factors.
**Blocker:** "I don't have time to evaluate another tool" (bandwidth). "How do you handle our data?" (security). "What happens if this vendor goes under?" (viability for a startup vendor). "My team tried [competitor] and it didn't work" (previous bad experience with legal tech).

### HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH THEM

**Tone:** Executive, concise, outcome-oriented. They have 15 minutes for you. Respect their time.
**Language:** "Legal function efficiency," "risk reduction," "cost per contract," "headcount avoidance," "board-ready metrics." They think in terms of the legal department's contribution to the business, not individual contract workflows.
**Avoid:** Feature-level detail in initial conversations (their team handles that). Anything that implies their current process is broken (they built it). Pressure tactics or artificial urgency (they have seen every sales tactic).
**Lead with:** Outcome data from a peer company ("GC at [Peer Company] reduced outside counsel spend by 30% and cut contract cycle time from 14 days to 3 days"). Business-level ROI, not feature demos.

### WHERE TO REACH THEM

**Primary:** Warm introduction from their champion (Senior Counsel). Peer referral from another GC. Executive events (ACC Chief Legal Officers Summit, invitation-only GC roundtables).
**Content:** They read Harvard Business Review, ACC Docket, The American Lawyer, and follow thought leadership from legal industry analysts. They are on LinkedIn but post infrequently.
**Timing:** Most receptive during annual budget planning (typically Q4 for calendar-year companies). Also receptive immediately after a trigger event when their team escalates the pain.

## BUYER PERSONA: The Efficiency-Minded CFO (Economic Buyer / Co-signer)

**Role:** Chief Financial Officer / VP Finance
================================================================

### WHO THEY ARE

The financial executive who controls budget allocation. They are not a legal professional and do not understand contract review workflows in detail, but they understand cost centres, headcount planning, and operational efficiency. They get involved in legal tool purchases when: (a) the cost exceeds the GC's unilateral approval authority, (b) the ROI story involves headcount avoidance (which directly affects their budget), or (c) the company is in a cost-optimisation phase. They are the person who asks "what's the ROI?" and "can we defer this to next quarter?"

### WHAT THEY WANT

**Primary goal:** Control operating costs while supporting growth. They are measured on operational efficiency ratios and budget adherence.
**Secondary goal:** Reduce reliance on outside counsel (a line item they can directly measure and cut).

### WHAT THEY FEAR

**Primary fear:** Approving spend that does not deliver measurable ROI. Being the person who approved a tool that the team stopped using after 6 months.
**Risk aversion:** SaaS sprawl -- another tool added to the stack that becomes shelfware. Wants clear usage metrics and a cancellation path.

### HOW THEY BUY

**Discovery:** They do not discover legal tools independently. They are brought into the process by the GC or champion.
**Evaluation:** They review the business case with a financial lens: cost vs. headcount savings, outside counsel spend reduction, contract cycle time impact on revenue (delayed contracts = delayed revenue).
**Decision:** They co-sign the purchase. Their approval is faster when the ROI case is pre-built with numbers they trust.
**Blocker:** "We're in a hiring freeze / cost-cutting mode" (budget constraint). "Show me the usage data from the pilot" (proof of adoption). "Can we start smaller?" (risk mitigation through phased rollout).

### HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH THEM

**Tone:** Data-driven, concise, financial. Speak in their language -- dollars, hours, headcount equivalents.
**Language:** "Headcount avoidance," "outside counsel spend reduction," "cost per contract reviewed," "time-to-revenue impact of contract delays," "payback period."
**Avoid:** Legal jargon. Feature details. Anything that sounds like a technology pitch rather than a business case.
**Lead with:** A one-page ROI model with their company's numbers plugged in (contracts per month x time saved per contract x average lawyer cost = annual savings).

### WHERE TO REACH THEM

**Primary:** Internal presentation from the GC/champion with a pre-built financial case. You will rarely reach the CFO directly through outbound.
**Content:** Not relevant for outbound -- they are an internal stakeholder to enable, not an outbound target.
**Timing:** Budget planning season (Q4) or immediately after a cost-cutting directive from the board.

## BDR QUALIFICATION FRAMEWORK

Use this checklist during discovery calls to quickly qualify or disqualify accounts:

### MUST HAVE (all 4 required to proceed)

- [ ] Company size: 300 -- 1,500 employees
- [ ] Legal team size: 3 -- 8 people
- [ ] Monthly contract volume: 200+ contracts
- [ ] No incumbent CLM platform with dedicated legal ops team managing it

### SHOULD HAVE (2 of 4 to prioritise)

- [ ] Recent Series B/C funding (last 12 months) OR recent M&A activity
- [ ] Active legal hiring (open roles for legal team members)
- [ ] Industry: Fintech, healthtech, or B2B SaaS
- [ ] Champion identified at Senior Counsel / VP Legal Ops level

### MUST NOT HAVE (any 1 is a disqualifier)

- [ ] 5,000+ employees with dedicated Legal Ops team of 5+
- [ ] Incumbent CLM platform (Ironclad, Icertis, Agiloft) with active dedicated admin
- [ ] Fewer than 50 employees or fewer than 2 legal team members
- [ ] Monthly contract volume under 100 (steady state, excluding one-time events)

### SCORING

- 4/4 Must Have + 3-4 Should Have + 0 Must Not Have = **TIER 1** -- assign top AE, fast-track demo
- 4/4 Must Have + 2 Should Have + 0 Must Not Have = **TIER 2** -- standard AE assignment
- 4/4 Must Have + 0-1 Should Have + 0 Must Not Have = **TIER 3** -- nurture, revisit quarterly
- Any Must Not Have present = **DISQUALIFY** -- add to marketing nurture only
