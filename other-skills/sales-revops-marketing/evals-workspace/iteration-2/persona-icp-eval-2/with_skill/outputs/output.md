BUYER PERSONA: The VP of Engineering -- Mid-Market B2B SaaS
Role: VP of Engineering / VP Engineering / Head of Engineering (200-1000 employee B2B SaaS companies)
================================================================

WHO THEY ARE

A seasoned engineering leader, typically 12-20 years into their career, who has progressed from individual contributor through senior engineering manager to VP. They manage 30-150 engineers across multiple squads and own the entire software delivery lifecycle -- from hiring and team structure to architecture decisions and shipping cadence. At a mid-market B2B SaaS company, they sit between the CTO (who sets technical vision) and senior engineering managers (who run day-to-day execution). They are measured on two things above all else: can the team ship reliably, and can the team retain its best people. They became a VP because they were the person who consistently delivered -- and they carry that builder identity into a role that is increasingly about organizational design, process, and cross-functional negotiation with Product and Sales. They are deeply technical, often still reviewing architecture decisions, and they are skeptical of anyone who talks about engineering productivity without understanding the nuances of their codebase, team structure, and deployment pipeline.

WHAT THEY WANT

Primary goal: Increase engineering velocity -- measured by deployment frequency, cycle time, and the ratio of feature work to maintenance/toil -- without burning out the team or compromising quality. They want to ship more with the team they have, not by hiring more.

Secondary goal: Reduce engineering attrition. They know that developer experience (DX) is the leading indicator of retention. If their best engineers are frustrated by slow CI/CD pipelines, flaky tests, unclear capacity planning, and tooling friction, those engineers will leave for companies that have solved these problems. A VP who keeps attrition below 10% in a competitive talent market looks exceptional to their CTO and CEO.

WHAT THEY FEAR

Primary fear: A failed tool rollout that creates more friction than it removes. They have seen (or led) a tool adoption that the team resisted, that added cognitive overhead, that became shelfware, and that made them look like they wasted budget and political capital. The phrase "my team will resist another tool in the stack" is not an objection -- it is a scar from a previous experience.

Risk aversion: They fear being the VP who bought something that engineers hate. Engineering teams are vocal. A poorly received tool becomes a meme in Slack channels within a week. The VP knows this and will insist on a proof of concept (POC) where their team actually uses the tool before committing budget. They also fear vendor claims that do not survive contact with their actual environment -- "10x productivity" claims trigger an immediate credibility alarm because they know productivity is contextual, hard to measure, and never 10x from a single tool.

HOW THEY BUY

Discovery: They discover tools through three channels, in order of trust: (1) peer recommendations from other VPs of Engineering in their network -- Slack communities like Rands Leadership Slack, LeadDev community, CTO Craft, or private engineering leadership groups; (2) content they encounter while solving a specific problem -- engineering blogs (InfoQ, The Pragmatic Engineer by Gergely Orosz, StaffEng.com, LeadDev articles), podcasts (The Engineering Manager podcast, Software Engineering Daily, Changelog), or conference talks; (3) inbound from their senior engineering managers who have evaluated the tool bottom-up. They almost never respond to cold outbound that leads with the product. They respond to outbound that demonstrates understanding of a problem they are currently facing.

Evaluation: Once interested, they delegate initial evaluation to a senior engineering manager (the champion in your sales cycle). The senior EM runs a technical evaluation and a POC with a real team on a real workflow. The VP reviews the POC results -- they want to see: (a) measurable improvement in a metric they care about (cycle time, build time, deployment frequency), (b) minimal friction in adoption (no steep learning curve, integrates with existing stack), and (c) positive team sentiment (did engineers actually like using it?). The POC is non-negotiable. Any attempt to skip it or rush it will kill the deal. They expect 2-4 weeks for a meaningful POC, not a 30-minute sandbox demo.

Decision: The VP of Engineering is typically the economic buyer who signs off on budget. For deals above $100K ARR, they may need CTO approval or a brief with the CFO. They will make the decision based on: POC results, team feedback, total cost of ownership (including integration and maintenance burden), and whether the vendor understands their world. They do not make impulse purchases. Expect a 60-90 day sales cycle from first meeting to signature at mid-market.

Blocker: "My team will resist another tool in the stack." This is the most common objection and it is real. Their team already uses GitHub/GitLab, Jira, CI/CD tooling, observability platforms, and multiple internal tools. Every new tool competes for attention and creates context-switching costs. To overcome this: (1) show that the tool replaces or consolidates existing tooling rather than adding to it, (2) present POC evidence from a comparable team where adoption was smooth, (3) propose a phased rollout starting with one team rather than a big-bang deployment, (4) frame the tool as something that reduces tool-stack complexity (fewer tabs, fewer dashboards, fewer manual processes) rather than adding another layer. Never argue with the objection -- validate it ("you're right, your team is already managing a lot of tools") and then reframe ("that's exactly why we built this to consolidate X, Y, and Z into one view rather than add another standalone tool").

HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH THEM

Tone: Peer-level technical. They can smell marketing from the first sentence. Speak as an engineer who happens to work at a vendor, not as a salesperson who memorized technical talking points. If you do not understand CI/CD pipelines, deployment strategies, or developer experience metrics, they will disengage immediately.

Language that resonates:
- "Cycle time," "deployment frequency," "DORA metrics," "developer experience"
- "Engineering capacity planning" (they think in terms of team capacity, not just individual productivity)
- "Reduce toil," "automate the undifferentiated work"
- "Here's what we measured in the POC" (data-first, always)
- "Your team's feedback after two weeks was..." (team sentiment matters to them)
- "We integrate with your existing stack" (reduces perceived switching cost)

Language that alienates -- NEVER USE:
- "10x productivity" or any multiplier claim -- triggers instant skepticism
- "Best-in-class," "world-class," "industry-leading" -- empty superlatives
- "Seamless integration" -- they know nothing is seamless; say "we have a native GitHub/GitLab integration that takes about 20 minutes to set up"
- "Platform," "solution," "leverage," "synergy" -- corporate jargon that signals you are a marketer, not a peer
- "Your developers will love it" -- they decide what their developers will love after a POC, not because you said so
- "ROI calculator" as a selling tool -- they trust their own data, not your spreadsheet

Avoid: Slide decks with customer logos and no substance. Feature dumps without context. Comparison charts that trash competitors. Any claim you cannot back with data from their POC or a comparable customer.

Lead with: Pain. Specifically: "I've talked to 20+ VPs of Engineering at companies your size and the pattern I keep hearing is [specific pain related to CI/CD bottlenecks, engineering capacity visibility, or developer toil]. Is that something you're dealing with too?" Then shut up and listen. The VP will tell you exactly how to sell to them if you ask the right question and listen to the answer.

WHERE TO REACH THEM

Primary channels:
- LinkedIn (they are active but not responsive to pitch-first InMail -- engage with their content before reaching out)
- Email (concise, specific, referencing a real problem -- 3 sentences maximum for the first touch)
- Warm introduction through their senior engineering managers (your champion), through VPs of Engineering at your existing customers, or through engineering leadership community connections

Content they consume:
- The Pragmatic Engineer (Gergely Orosz) -- newsletter and blog
- InfoQ -- architecture and engineering leadership content
- LeadDev -- articles and conference talks on engineering management
- StaffEng.com -- for senior IC and management career content
- Software Engineering Daily, Changelog -- podcasts
- Hacker News -- for technical deep-dives and industry signal
- Conference talks from QCon, LeadDev conferences (London, New York, Berlin), KubeCon (if infrastructure-adjacent), and SREcon

Events they attend:
- QCon (London, San Francisco) -- architecture and engineering leadership
- LeadDev conferences -- engineering management focused
- CTO Summit and CTO Craft events -- peer networking
- Local engineering leadership meetups in major tech hubs

Timing -- when they are most receptive:
- Immediately after a production incident caused by CI/CD pipeline failure or deployment process breakdown (the pain is fresh)
- During annual or quarterly planning when they are sizing team capacity vs. commitments (they realize they cannot hire their way out)
- After losing a senior engineer who cited developer experience or tooling frustration in their exit interview
- When their company announces a growth milestone (new funding round, new product line) that requires scaling engineering output without proportional headcount growth
- Budget planning cycles: typically Q4 for the following year, or Q1 reallocation at mid-market companies

## ACTIONABLE OUTREACH GUIDANCE

**First-touch strategy (for reps building outreach from this persona):**

Do NOT lead with the product. Lead with a pattern you have observed across their peer group.

Example opening for email (adapt to specific prospect research):
"[Name], I've been talking to VPs of Engineering at [similar companies] and the consistent pattern is: deployment frequency is up but cycle time hasn't improved because the CI/CD pipeline has become the bottleneck. [Specific observation about their company if available]. Curious whether that matches what you're seeing."

Example opening for LinkedIn (under 100 words):
"[Name], saw your post about [specific topic]. I've been hearing from engineering leaders at [comparable companies] that [specific pain point]. We built [product category description, not product name] specifically around that problem. Would it be useful to share what we measured in a recent POC with a team similar to yours?"

**Sequence strategy:**
- Touch 1: Pain-first email referencing a specific, verifiable observation about their company or role
- Touch 2 (3 days later): LinkedIn connection request with a 1-sentence note referencing the email topic
- Touch 3 (5 days later): Share a relevant piece of content (engineering blog post, case study with metrics) -- no ask, just value
- Touch 4 (7 days later): Direct ask for a 20-minute call, framed as "I'd like to understand how you're handling [specific problem] -- happy to share what we've seen work at similar companies whether or not there's a fit"
- If no response after 4 touches: pause for 30 days, then re-engage only if a new timing signal appears (funding round, job posting, leadership change)

**Key POC framing (for when the deal advances):**
- Propose a 2-3 week POC with one team on one real workflow
- Define success metrics upfront with the champion (senior EM): cycle time reduction, build time improvement, developer satisfaction score
- Weekly check-in during POC -- not to sell, but to remove friction
- At POC conclusion: present results in the VP's language (team metrics + team sentiment), not in your product's language
