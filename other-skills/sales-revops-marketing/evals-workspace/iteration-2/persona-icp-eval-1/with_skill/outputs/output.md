# IDEAL CUSTOMER PROFILE -- AI Contract Review Tool

## FIRMOGRAPHIC CRITERIA

**Company size:** 200--1,000 employees
**Revenue range:** $20M--$500M ARR (mid-market)
**Stage:** Established growth-stage or mature mid-market companies; post-Series B through pre-IPO
**Geography:** Primary: United States, Canada. Secondary: UK, EU (English-speaking or bilingual legal teams)
**Industry (ranked by conversion signal strength):**
1. B2B SaaS companies (high contract volume from customer agreements, vendor agreements, and partner deals)
2. Fintech / financial services (regulatory pressure creates urgency; high contract complexity)
3. Professional services -- consulting, staffing, managed services (volume-driven; many client engagements = many contracts)
4. Healthcare IT and healthtech (compliance-heavy; HIPAA/BAA volume)
5. E-commerce / marketplace platforms (vendor and seller agreements at scale)

**Technology maturity:** Mid-tech -- uses modern SaaS stack (Slack, Google Workspace or M365, cloud-based tools) but does NOT yet have an entrenched Contract Lifecycle Management (CLM) platform. May use basic shared drives, spreadsheets, or lightweight tools like PandaDoc/DocuSign CLM lite for contract storage.

**Legal team size:** Minimum 3 in-house legal professionals (counsel, paralegals, contract managers). Sweet spot is 3--10. Below 3 typically lacks budget; above 10 often has CLM already.

**Contract volume:** Processes 500+ contracts per month across all types (customer, vendor, NDA, amendment, renewal).

# BUYER PERSONA: "The Scaling Legal Leader"

**Role:** Head of Legal, VP of Legal, General Counsel, Director of Legal Operations
================================================================

## WHO THEY ARE

A mid-career legal professional (8--15 years experience) who has transitioned from law firm practice into an in-house role at a growing technology or services company. They were hired or promoted specifically because the company's legal function needed to scale beyond ad hoc processes. They manage a small team (3--10 people) and are acutely aware that their team cannot keep pace with contract volume through manual review alone. They are operationally minded for a lawyer -- they think in terms of throughput, risk reduction, and team leverage, not just legal doctrine. They report to the CEO, COO, or CFO and feel pressure to demonstrate that legal is a business enabler, not a bottleneck.

## WHAT THEY WANT

**Primary goal:** Reduce contract review turnaround time and risk exposure without proportionally growing headcount. They are measured on how fast legal can review and clear contracts so deals close on time.

**Secondary goal:** Demonstrate to the executive team that the legal function is operationally sophisticated and data-driven. They want to move legal's internal reputation from "the department that slows things down" to "the team that protects revenue and accelerates deals."

## WHAT THEY FEAR

**Primary fear:** A contract slips through with unfavourable terms, an auto-renewal is missed, or a compliance clause is absent -- and the company suffers a material financial or regulatory consequence on their watch. They have seen this happen (or it already happened, which is why they are looking).

**Risk aversion:** They worry about adopting a tool that their team will not use (shelfware), that will require significant change management, or that will produce inaccurate analysis that the team then needs to double-check anyway (negating the time savings). They also worry about data security -- contracts contain highly sensitive business information and they need confidence in SOC 2 compliance, data handling, and access controls.

## HOW THEY BUY

**Discovery:** Peer referrals from other in-house counsel (ACC community, legal ops Slack groups, LinkedIn connections). Legal technology review sites (G2, Gartner peer reviews). Legal operations conferences (CLOC, ACC Annual Meeting, Legalweek). LinkedIn content from legal ops thought leaders. They do NOT respond well to cold outreach that feels salesy.

**Evaluation:** They want to see the product work on THEIR contracts, not a generic demo. They will push for a pilot or proof-of-concept with real documents. They trust peer references from similar-sized companies in similar industries more than case studies from enterprises. They will involve their team early -- if the paralegals and contract managers do not like the UX, the deal is dead.

**Decision:** Final budget approval comes from CFO or COO. The legal leader is the champion and must build the internal business case. The decision trigger is usually one of: (a) a specific incident that crystallised the pain, (b) the realisation during annual planning that headcount requests will be denied and they need a tooling alternative, or (c) a peer recommendation that creates urgency.

**Blocker:** "We're not ready for this yet" (prioritisation), "I need to see ROI before I can pitch this to the CFO" (business case gap), "Our contracts are too complex/unique for AI" (trust gap), "We need to focus on [other legal initiative] first" (competing priorities).

## HOW TO COMMUNICATE WITH THEM

**Tone:** Peer-level and substantive. They are a lawyer -- they appreciate precision, specificity, and intellectual honesty. Do not oversell or make absolute claims. Acknowledge limitations. Speak as a knowledgeable peer, not as a vendor pitching a product.

**Language that resonates:** "Contract turnaround time," "review throughput," "risk visibility," "clause-level analysis," "legal team leverage," "time-to-signature," "renewal tracking," "obligation management." Frame the tool as augmenting their team's judgment, not replacing it.

**Language that alienates:** "AI-powered" used as a buzzword without substance, "revolutionise your legal department," "eliminate the need for lawyers," "seamless integration" (they know nothing is seamless), "best-in-class" (meaningless), "cutting-edge" (they care about reliability, not novelty). Any language that implies legal review can be fully automated will destroy credibility.

**Avoid:** Generic ROI calculators that feel made up. Demos that only show simple contracts (they will test you on complexity). Marketing emails that reference "digital transformation." Any implication that their current process is bad or stupid -- they built that process and it works, it just does not scale.

**Lead with:** Specific pain point (missed renewal cost, review backlog days, compliance gap). Then peer reference ("Here is how a [similar company] handled the same problem"). Then a concrete, low-commitment next step.

## WHERE TO REACH THEM

**Primary channels:**
- LinkedIn (they are active; post and engage with legal ops content)
- Email (direct, short, specific -- NOT sequences that feel automated)
- Events: CLOC Institute, ACC Annual Meeting, Legalweek, Legal Ops conferences, local ACC chapter events
- Peer referral / warm introduction from existing customer or mutual connection

**Content they consume:**
- Artificial Lawyer (publication)
- CLOC newsletter and community
- ACC Docket
- LinkedIn posts from legal operations leaders (Mary O'Carroll, Connie Brenton, Stephanie Corey)
- Legal Operations podcasts (Technically Legal, Reinventing Professionals)
- G2 and Gartner peer reviews when actively evaluating

**Timing for outreach:**
- Highest receptivity: Q4 (Oct--Nov) during annual planning when they are building next year's budget and tooling roadmap
- Secondary: Q1 (Jan--Feb) when new budgets are allocated and new leaders are onboarding
- Trigger-based: Within 2 weeks of a job change, funding announcement, or legal ops hire
- Day/time: Tuesday--Thursday, early morning (before their day fills with contract reviews and meetings)

## ANTI-ICP SUMMARY: THREE PROFILES TO DISQUALIFY FAST

### 1. The Enterprise Rip-and-Replace

**Profile:** 1,000+ employees, existing CLM platform (Ironclad, Icertis, Agiloft), established legal ops team that selected and implemented their current tool.

**Why they waste time:** They are intellectually interested and will engage deeply in evaluation, but the switching cost (financial, political, operational) is massive. Average evaluation cycle: 6--12 months. Win rate: very low. Even if they like the product, the internal champion must justify abandoning a platform they spent 12+ months implementing.

**Disqualification signal:** During discovery, ask "What are you using for contract management today?" If the answer is a major CLM platform, qualify out unless they explicitly state the platform is failing and they have executive mandate to replace it.

### 2. The Solo Counsel Startup

**Profile:** <100 employees, single legal counsel (often part-time or outsourced), <100 contracts/month, pre-Series A or bootstrapped.

**Why they waste time:** Genuine pain but no budget, no procurement process, and no internal champion who can push a purchase through. They will request a free trial, use it briefly, and either churn or never convert from free to paid.

**Disqualification signal:** During qualification, ask "How many people are on your legal team?" If the answer is 1 (or "we use outside counsel"), qualify out immediately. Do not offer a pilot.

### 3. The E-Signature Shopper

**Profile:** Any size company that is actually looking for electronic signature capability, not contract review intelligence. They found you through search terms like "contract software" or "contract automation" but their need is document signing, not clause analysis.

**Why they waste time:** Product mismatch. They will sign up for a demo, realise the product does not do what they expected, and leave. Short time cost per lead but high volume -- this segment can consume significant SDR time.

**Disqualification signal:** In the inbound form or first SDR call, ask "What is the primary problem you are trying to solve?" If the answer involves "getting contracts signed faster" or "collecting signatures," redirect them to DocuSign/HelloSign and exit.
