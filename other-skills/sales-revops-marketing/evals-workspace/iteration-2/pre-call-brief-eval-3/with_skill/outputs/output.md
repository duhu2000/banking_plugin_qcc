DEAL HEALTH ASSESSMENT: AT RISK

Reasoning: Five concurrent warning signals make this assessment unambiguous:
1. **Adoption decline** (84% to 61%) is the strongest leading indicator of churn in any SaaS relationship -- a 23-point drop in two quarters is not noise, it is a trend
2. **Executive sponsor disengagement** -- Rachel Dunn missing three consecutive check-ins means you have lost your political air cover at the CISO level
3. **SLA breach** -- the P1 incident at 9 hours (2.25x the 4-hour SLA) creates a factual talking point for anyone building a case to switch vendors

5. **Competitive targeting** -- Sentinel Shield and CyberArch are not running generic ads; they are targeting Meridian specifically, which suggests they may have intelligence on the account's vulnerability

Any two of these factors together would warrant concern. All five together place this account firmly at risk. The 22-month runway to renewal is misleading -- the real decision window is the next 60-90 days while Whitfield's audit is active. If the audit concludes negatively, the remaining contract term becomes a countdown to exit, not a retention opportunity.

RISK FACTORS

1. **The CFO audit is your most urgent threat.** James Whitfield has no relationship with you, no memory of the original value proposition, and is evaluating pure cost-to-value. If his audit concludes before you establish a relationship and reframe the value narrative, you lose the ability to influence the outcome. He will evaluate you on paper -- and on paper, you have declining adoption and an SLA breach.

2. **Adoption decline without a diagnosed cause is undefendable.** If someone in the QBR asks "why has usage dropped 23 points?" and you cannot answer with specificity, you signal that you are not monitoring or caring about the account. Worse, if the drop is because teams have started using a competing tool, the switch may already be underway.

3. **Loss of executive sponsor access makes recovery harder.** Without Rachel Dunn in the room, you are navigating this through Marcus Tillman, who is operationally valuable but does not control budget or strategic vendor decisions. If Rachel has already mentally moved on, your channel to decision-making authority is broken.

RECOMMENDED NEXT ACTIONS

**Before the QBR (preparation -- do these immediately):**

1. **Get the adoption data segmented.** Do not walk in with "61%" as a single number. Break it down: which teams dropped off, which features lost usage, when exactly did the decline start (before or after the P1 incident?). If the decline correlates with the November incident, that is a different conversation than if it started independently.

2. **Prepare a P1 incident retrospective.** Bring a one-page root cause analysis and the specific remediation steps taken since November. Include any SLA credit or goodwill gesture if one was offered. If none was offered, consider proactively offering one in the QBR -- it demonstrates accountability.

3. **Pre-brief with Marcus Tillman before the QBR.** Call Marcus 48-72 hours before the QBR. Ask directly: "What is the temperature in the room? What is James going to ask about? Is Rachel planning to attend?" Marcus is your ally -- use this call to gather intelligence, not to sell. Ask him what would make this QBR a success from his perspective.

4. **Research James Whitfield.** Review his LinkedIn profile, previous roles, public statements. Understand his background -- if he came from a cost-cutting mandate at a previous company, prepare for a value-justification conversation. If he has a security background, prepare a risk-reduction narrative.

5. **Prepare a competitive comparison.** Have a factual, non-disparaging comparison ready for Sentinel Shield and CyberArch. Focus on switching costs, integration complexity, and risk-of-change -- not feature lists. The goal is to make the cost of switching feel higher than the cost of staying.

**During the QBR:**

1. **Open with accountability on the P1 incident.** Do not wait for it to be raised. Say: "Before we get into the review, I want to address the November incident directly. Here is what happened, why it happened, and the three specific changes we made to prevent recurrence." Leading with this disarms the room and builds credibility.

2. **Present a usage recovery plan, not a usage report.** Do not show the 61% number and hope no one notices the decline. Present it as: "Adoption moved from 84% to 61%. We have diagnosed [X] as the primary driver. Here is our proposed 90-day recovery plan with specific milestones." This reframes the conversation from "you are failing" to "we have a plan."

3. **Introduce yourself to James Whitfield with a value narrative, not a product pitch.** His frame is cost-to-value. Your opening with him should be: "I understand you are reviewing vendor relationships -- I welcome that. Here is the business case for our partnership in terms you can take back to your analysis: [risk reduced, incidents prevented, compliance maintained, cost of replacement]."

4. **Directly address Rachel Dunn's absence.** If she is not in the QBR, ask Marcus (or raise it diplomatically): "We have missed Rachel in our last few sessions. I want to make sure we are aligned at the executive level -- can we schedule a 20-minute check-in with her in the next two weeks?" Do not let her absence become the new normal.

NEXT STEP TO BOOK

At the close of the QBR, book two specific follow-ups:

1. **A 30-minute meeting with James Whitfield within two weeks** -- positioned as "I want to make sure you have everything you need for your vendor review, and I would rather give you the data directly than have you work from incomplete information." This gives you a seat at the audit table.

2. **A 20-minute executive check-in with Rachel Dunn within three weeks** -- positioned through Marcus as "We want to share our 90-day usage recovery plan and get her input on strategic priorities for the next year." This re-engages your executive sponsor.

Do not leave the QBR without both of these on the calendar.

THINGS NOT TO DO

1. **Do not go in defensive.** If you walk into the QBR trying to explain away the adoption drop and the SLA breach, you will lose the room. Lead with accountability and a forward-looking plan. Defence invites attack; ownership invites partnership.

2. **Do not ignore James Whitfield or treat him as a finance obstacle.** He is a new decision-maker who will influence whether this contract renews. Treat him as a new champion to win, not a threat to manage. If you dismiss him, he will dismiss you in his audit.

3. **Do not badmouth Sentinel Shield or CyberArch.** If competitors come up, respond with: "I am familiar with both. Happy to walk through a factual comparison if that would be useful for your evaluation. What I can tell you is what switching would actually involve from an integration and timeline perspective." Let switching costs do the work.

4. **Do not present adoption data without a diagnosis and recovery plan.** Showing a declining graph without an explanation and a fix makes you look like you are reporting a problem you have not tried to solve. Data without a plan is an indictment.

5. **Do not treat the 22-month runway as safety.** The renewal decision will be made in the next 90 days during the CFO's audit, not in 22 months. Every action should be calibrated to that compressed timeline.

Pivot to: "Switching security platforms mid-contract carries integration risk and a 6-9 month ramp period where coverage gaps are most dangerous. What specific capability are they offering that you feel is missing from what we provide today?" Force the conversation into specifics -- generic "better platform" claims dissolve under scrutiny.
